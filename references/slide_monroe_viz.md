# Slide Pattern G: Monroe's Visualization Slide Pattern

> **Note:** The `---` dividers in this file are documentation section boundaries. When generating presentation output, use `---` only as slide boundary markers per SKILL.md format rules.

## Pattern Overview

**Designation:** Pattern G (Layer 2 -- Slide-Level Pattern)
**Concept:** Create a vivid picture of the future with or without your proposed solution, leveraging mental imagery and loss aversion to motivate action.
**Origin:** Alan H. Monroe's Motivated Sequence (1930s) -- specifically the Visualization step, the critical differentiator between informative and persuasive presentations.
**Best For:** Creating urgency, change management, motivating executive decisions, overcoming complacency or status-quo bias, fundraising and advocacy.

**When to Use:**
- The audience faces a genuine fork in the road -- act or do nothing
- You need to translate abstract data into felt consequences
- A decision has been deferred and you need to break the stalemate
- The audience understands the problem intellectually but has not internalized the urgency emotionally

**When NOT to Use:**
- The decision is already made and the audience is aligned -- use a roadmap or action slide
- There is no meaningful negative consequence of inaction (the pattern will feel forced)
- The audience is hostile and skeptical of emotional appeals -- lead with evidence first
- The scenarios cannot be grounded in credible data or projections

---

## Core Philosophy

**Make the future feel real.**

People do not act on abstractions. They act on vivid mental images of what their world will look like tomorrow. Monroe's Visualization exploits two psychological truths:

1. **Mental simulation drives decision-making.** When people can see themselves in a future scenario, they form an emotional commitment that data alone cannot create. The brain treats vividly imagined futures almost identically to remembered experiences.
2. **Loss aversion outweighs gain anticipation.** Losses feel roughly twice as painful as equivalent gains feel good. The WITHOUT scenario often carries more motivational weight than the WITH scenario.

**Why specificity matters:** "Things will get better" motivates no one. "Your sales team opens their CRM Monday morning to find every lead pre-scored and pre-routed -- cutting prep time from 45 minutes to 5" creates a mental image the audience can inhabit.

The most effective visualization slides simultaneously: ground the scenario in a real data point (credibility), describe in present tense (immediacy), and connect to the audience's stated goals or fears (relevance).

**The Monday morning test:** Can the audience picture what their Monday morning looks like in this future? If yes, the visualization is vivid enough. If no, you are still writing abstractions.

## Three Visualization Approaches

### Approach 1: Positive Only
**When to Use:** Audience already feels the pain; aspirational contexts (vision talks, team rallies); negative framing would feel redundant or demoralizing.
**Structure:** Future State (vivid, present-tense) + Tangible Outcomes (3 quantified benefits) + What It Feels Like (experiential description).

### Approach 2: Negative Only
**When to Use:** Audience is complacent; regulatory/safety/compliance contexts where inaction is catastrophic; the positive future is obvious once urgency is accepted.
**Structure:** If We Do Nothing (projected deterioration, time-bound) + Cascading Consequences (first/second/third-order effects) + The Human Cost (personal impact on specific roles).

### Approach 3: Contrast (WITH / WITHOUT) -- Default
**When to Use:** Default choice for most situations. Audience is undecided or mixed. Sales presentations, executive decisions, board approvals, change management.
**Structure:** WITH (3 positive outcomes) side by side with WITHOUT (3 negative outcomes) + Bottom Line (single sentence crystallizing the stakes).

| Context | Recommended Approach |
|---------|---------------------|
| Board approval for investment | **Contrast** |
| Post-incident safety talk | **Positive Only** |
| Complacent team quarterly review | **Negative Only** |
| Sales pitch to prospect | **Contrast** |
| Vision keynote, team rally | **Positive Only** |
| Compliance / regulatory advocacy | **Negative Only** |

```
+------------------------------+-------------------------------+

|                              |                               |
| [Positive outcome 1]        | [Negative outcome 1]          |
|   - Quantified metric        |   - Quantified metric         |
|                              |                               |
| [Positive outcome 2]        | [Negative outcome 2]          |
|   - Quantified metric        |   - Quantified metric         |
|                              |                               |
| [Positive outcome 3]        | [Negative outcome 3]          |
|   - Quantified metric        |   - Quantified metric         |
|                              |                               |
+------------------------------+-------------------------------+
| BOTTOM LINE: [Single sentence crystallizing the stakes]       |
+--------------------------------------------------------------+
```

**Alternative layouts:** Vertical Split (WITH on top, WITHOUT below -- for emphasis on positive), Timeline Fork (today splits into two quarterly-milestone paths), Positive-Only (future state + tangible outcomes + what it feels like), Negative-Only Cascade (consequences compounding over time with timestamps).

## Detailed Element Guide

### The WITH Scenario

- **Be specific and concrete.** Not "better results" but "40% faster processing, zero manual errors, 15 hours reclaimed per analyst per week."
- **Use present tense.** "Your team opens their dashboard to real-time data" not "would open." Present tense makes the future feel like it is already happening.
- **Quantify where possible.** Numbers create credibility; narrative creates emotion. You need both.
- **Connect to the audience's stated goals.** Mirror their language and their KPIs.
- **Show the experience, not just the metric.** "NPS rises to 72" plus "your CS team starts getting unsolicited referrals."
- **Keep to 3-4 outcomes.** More than 4 dilutes the mental image.

### The WITHOUT Scenario

- **Be honest, not manipulative.** Every claim must be a credible projection based on observable trends. Exaggeration destroys trust.
- **Quantify risks and costs.** "$4.7M in revenue at risk" not "significant revenue risk."
- **Show cascading consequences.** First-order triggers second-order triggers third-order. This demonstrates you have thought through the full impact.
- **Make it personal to the audience's role.** CFO: margin compression. VP Engineering: tech debt and burnout. Sales leader: competitive positioning.
- **Use credible projections.** "At the current rate of 12% quarterly decline..." Sourced projections are harder to dismiss.
- **Do not stack unrelated negatives.** Every negative must be a consequence of the same inaction.

### The Title

- State the outcome, the fork, or the contrast -- not the process
- Include a timeframe when possible (creates urgency)
- Make the preferred path clear, even in a contrast slide

### Bottom Line Sentence (for Contrast slides)

- One sentence that crystallizes the stakes and makes the choice clear
- Should quantify the gap between WITH and WITHOUT: "The difference is a $20M swing and a 60-day window"
- Write this sentence first, then build the two columns to support it
- The bottom line should feel inevitable after reading both columns -- not like a new claim

### Emotional Calibration

- For every claim, have a data source you could cite if challenged
- If you find yourself using "devastating" or "revolutionary," ask whether the data supports that intensity
- The audience should leave feeling "we must act" -- not "we are being sold to"
- Ground every positive claim in pilot data, benchmarks, or conservative projections
- Match emotional intensity to the actual stakes -- understating is more persuasive than overstating

## Title Writing Guide

### Title Formulas

**Fork/Choice:** `[By timeframe], [audience] either [positive path] or [negative path]`
Example: "By Q4, your team either leads the category or spends 18 months catching up"

**Consequence:** `Without action, [specific negative outcome] [by timeframe]`
Example: "Without action, we enter 2027 with a $3.1M revenue gap and 40% fewer senior engineers"

### DO Examples

1. "Two paths forward: market leadership or competitive irrelevance"
2. "By Q4, your team either leads the category or spends 18 months catching up"
3. "With this platform, your analysts reclaim 15 hours per week and zero reports are late"
4. "Without action, we enter 2027 with a $3.1M revenue gap and 40% fewer senior engineers"

### DON'T Examples

1. "Future Scenarios" -- tells the audience nothing; a label
2. "Potential Outcomes" -- academic framing that drains urgency
3. "Benefits of the Solution" -- describes the slide type, not the future state
4. "Pros and Cons" -- frames the decision as balanced when the purpose is to persuade

| Audience | WITH Emphasizes | WITHOUT Emphasizes | Tone | Layout |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Executive** | Revenue, margin, competitive positioning, ROI | Revenue at risk, market share erosion, cost escalation | Data-driven, strategic, concise | Side-by-side or timeline fork |
| **Technical** | Performance benchmarks, automation, developer experience | Technical debt, scaling failures, talent attrition | Precise, metric-heavy, jargon-appropriate | Side-by-side with metrics |
| **Sales / Prospects** | What prospect's world looks like as your customer | What happens with competitor, delay, or status quo | Confident, personalized | Side-by-side contrast |
| **Internal / Change Mgmt** | Daily work improvement, less friction, better tools | Current frustrations worsen, burnout, talent leaving | Empathetic, practical, authentic | Vertical split or positive-only |

## Common Mistakes

1. **Vague scenarios.** "Things will improve" or "risks will increase." Fix: replace with specific, quantified, present-tense scenarios.
2. **Unrealistic positive scenario (overselling).** "Revenue doubles in 90 days." Fix: use conservative projections and reference pilot results.
3. **Fear-mongering negative scenario.** Apocalyptic consequences disproportionate to risk. Fix: ground in observable data; let the data create the alarm.
4. **Mismatched dimensions.** WITH talks about satisfaction while WITHOUT talks about regulatory risk. Fix: mirror every dimension across both columns.
5. **Missing quantification.** "Faster processing" instead of "40% faster." Fix: attach a number to every claim.
6. **Overcrowded slide.** Six outcomes per side plus narrative. Fix: max 3-4 outcomes per scenario, one bottom-line sentence.
7. **Past tense instead of present tense.** "Revenue would have increased" makes the future hypothetical. Fix: use present tense throughout.

## Quality Checklist

- [ ] Every outcome is specific enough to create a mental image
- [ ] Present tense is used throughout both scenarios
- [ ] At least one outcome per scenario includes a quantified metric
- [ ] Every WITH claim is traceable to pilot data, case studies, or conservative projections
- [ ] Every WITHOUT claim is grounded in observable trends or credible forecasts
- [ ] WITH and WITHOUT scenarios contrast the same dimensions (mirrored structure)
- [ ] Maximum 3-4 outcomes per scenario
- [ ] A bottom-line sentence synthesizes the stakes (for contrast slides)
- [ ] Title states the choice, the preferred future, or the key contrast
- [ ] Negative scenarios are honest, not manipulative
- [ ] Positive scenarios are aspirational but achievable
- [ ] Every WITH outcome is directly enabled by the proposed solution

## Pro Tips

1. **Use present tense to make scenarios feel immediate.** "Your team opens the dashboard to real-time data" is viscerally different from "would open." Present tense triggers mental simulation; conditional tense keeps it hypothetical.
2. **Lead with the negative scenario -- loss aversion makes it stickier.** Present WITHOUT first; the audience processes the loss, then experiences relief when the positive alternative appears. This sequence leverages the psychological principle that losses feel twice as intense as equivalent gains.
3. **Ground every scenario in a real data point.** "Based on our Q1 pilot..." or "At the current quarterly decline rate of 12%..." One credible anchor makes the entire slide defensible. Without a data anchor, visualization becomes speculation.
4. **Mirror dimensions exactly in contrast slides.** If WITH mentions "time to close," WITHOUT must mention "time to close." Mismatched dimensions weaken the comparison.
5. **Write the bottom-line sentence first.** "The difference between these two futures is a $20M swing and a 60-day window." Then work backward to build the contrast.
6. **Personalize to the audience's role, not your role.** Describe what the audience's world looks like, not what your product does.
7. **Use the "Monday morning test."** Can the audience picture what their Monday morning looks like in this future? If yes, the visualization is vivid enough.
